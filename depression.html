Depression

Depression is a common and serious medical illness that negatively affects how you feel, the way you think and how you act. Depression causes feelings of sadness and/or a loss of interest in activities once enjoyed. Depression can also lead to a variety of emotional and physical problems and can decrease a person's ability to function at work and at home. Currently, depression affects an estimated one in 15 adults (6.7%) in any given year, according the American Psychiatric Association. Likewise, one in six people (16.6%) will experience depression. It is important to know that depression can affect anyone; even a person who appears to live in ideal circumstances. However, some of the several factors which can play a role in depression, include biochemestry, genetics, personality, and environmental factors. Symptoms in depression include:

  • A change in eating or sleeping habits.
  • Feeling tired all the time.
  • A loss of interest in people and/or activities that once brought you pleasure.
  • Difficult concentration, remembering or making decisions.
  • Restleness or irritability.
  • Thinking about death or suicide.
  • Feeling of worthlessness and guilt.
  • Feeling of helplessness and hopelessness.

Types of Mental Health Treatments

Before a diagnosis or treatment is completed for someone with depression, a psychotherapist, and a psychiatrist, conduct a through diagnosticDepression evaluation, including an interview and possibly a blood test to assure that depression is not due to a medical condition, such as a thyroid problem. The purpose of this evaluation is to identify specific symptoms, medical, family history, cultural factors and environmental factors, to arrive at a diagnosis and plan of treatment. Psychotherapy, or "talk therapy" is sometimes used alone for treatment of mild depression; for moderate to severe depression, psychotherapy is often used along with antidepressant medications, prescribed by a psychiatrist. Psychiatrists usually recommend patients to continue taking medications for six or more months, until after the symptoms have improved. Psychotherapy may involve therapy with only the individual, but it can also include other family members, or friends. Group therapy is also included in the treatment plan, involving patient with similar mental illness.


Cognitive Behavioral Therapy

Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT) is a type of psychotherapy that works with a patient's thought patterns. It helps to change a patient's behaviors and moods. CBT treatment for depression is a blend of cognitive and behavioral therapy, whereby the psychotherapist helps a patient to identify particular negative thought patterns as well as the patient's responses to stressful and challenging situations, which could be triggering his/her depression.

How depression is treated with CBT:

Cognitive Behavioral Therapy, unlike other approaches to therapy, is a short-term approach that usually takes a little as six sessions; it can also extends up to 20-25 therapy sessions. Througout each session, the psychotherapist and the patient, Depressionwill look at identifying situations that may be contributing to or causing a patient's depression. At this time, any distorted perceptions and current pattern of thinking are identified and reviewed with patient. Often, patients are encouraged to keep a journal to record different life events and reactions to those events. This list then helps the psychotherapist to identify and break down the patient's thoughts and reactions into different categories of negative thoughts, including:
  • Overgeneralization - coming to a general conclusion based on a single incident or piece of evidence.
  • Filtering - taking the negative details and magnify them while filtering out all positive aspects of situation.
  • Catastrophizing - expecting disaster; noticing or hearing about a problem and starting "what if's" statments.
  • Emotional reasoning - believing that what you feel must be true - automatically. If you feel stupid and boring, then you must be stupid and boring.
  • Polarized thinking - things are black and white, good or bad. having to be perfect or else, you are a failure. There is no middle ground.
Psychotherapists assist patients in challenging their negative thoughts. To challenge cognitive distortions, patients must first become aware of their thinking, and then be willing to entertain the idea that some of those thoughts might be distorted or untrue. The next step involves retructuring thoughts. This session involves in helping patients dispute the distored thoughts and replacing them with a more balanced, helpful, and realistic way of thinking. What is the repetitive thought? List emotions that you feel when thinking those thoughts. Is there a cognitive distortion(s) in your thoughts? Think of a more rational response to Cognitive distortion.
Ex: "I know I'm going to fail that exam." Anxious, depressive, discourage. Fortune telling/predicting the future. "Actually, I've passed many exams before and I'm pretty confident that if I study enough, I'll do fine."
In addition to learning about common thought patterns and how they impact a patient's life, the above example can also serve as a way to chip away at the ill effects that distorted thinking causes. In turn, this process is designed to strengthen that muscles of rational, broad-based, and self-supportive thinking.
