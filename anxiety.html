Anxiety

Experiencing occasional anxiety is a normal part of life. However, people with anxiety didorders frequently have intense, excessive and persitent worry and fear about everyday situations. Often, anxiety disorders involve repeated episodes of suddent feelings of intense anxiety and fear that reach a peak within minutes to what is called panic attacks. according the Anxiety and Depression Association of America, anxiety disorders are the most common mental illness in the U.S., affecting 40 million adults in the United States age 18 and older, or 18.1% of the population every year. Examples of anxiety disorders include generalized anxiety disorder, social anxiety disorder, specific phobias and separation anxiety disorder. Common anxiety signs and symptoms include:
  • Feeling nervous, restless or tense
  • Feeling weak or tired
  • Having a sense of impending danger, panic or doom
  • Having an increased heart rate
  • Having trouble sleeping
  • Having difficulty controlling worry
  • Having the urge to avoid things that trigger anxiety
  • Breathing rapidly (Hyperventilation)
When it comes to treating anxiety disorders, research shows that therapy is usually the most effective option. For example, the Center for Treatment of Anxiety and Mood disorders reports that In-vivo (Exposure) therapy, have proven to have more lasting results than psychotropic medications. That is because anxiety therapy - unlike anxiety medication - treats more than just the symptoms of the problem. Therapy can help to uncover the underlining causes of worries and fears; to learn how to look at situations in new, less frightening ways; and develop better coping and problem-solving skills.
When someone goes through psychotherapy, they learn what triggers their reaction to specific fears, places, or situations DepressionTalk therapies help people overcome these triggers so they can have a better quality of life. Furthermore, these methods teach skills that are lifelong. This allows the person to not only feel better, but gives them something to fall back on if their stressors return at some point in the future.


Exposure Therapy for Anxiety:

Exposure therapy, as the name suggests, exposes a patient to the situations or objects that he/she fears. The idea is that through repeated exposures, the patient will feel an increasing sense of control over the situation and consequently his/her anxiety will diminish. The exposure is done in one of two ways: the psychotherapist may ask the patient to imagine the scary situation, or he/she may confront it in real life. Exposure therapy may be used alone, or it may be conducted as part of CBT(Cognitive Behavior Therapy).
Exposure therapy employs several methods to support the reduction or troubling emotional responses. Psychotherapists conduct assessments to determine what combination of techniques will prove most effective. Types of exposure includes:
  • Imaginal Exposure: In this type of exposure, the patient in therapy is asked to mentally confront the fear situatio by picturing it in his/her mind. For example, a patient with agoraphobia, a fear of crowded places, might imagine standing in a crowded mall.
  • In Vivo Exposure: Here a patient is exposed to real-life objects and scenarios. For example, a patient with a fear of flying might be asked to to to the airport and watch a place take off.
  • Virtual Reality Exposure: This type of exposure combines elements of both imaginal and in vivo exposure so that a patient is placed in situations that appear real but are actually fabricated. For example, a patient who has a fear of heights - acrophobia - might participate in a virtual simulation of climbing of the stairs of a tall building.

How anxiety is treated with Exposure Therapy; Overcoming Your Fear of Flying - An Example.

Rather than facing the patient's biggest fear right away, which can be traumatizing, exposure therapy usually starts with a situation that is only midly threatening and works up from there. This step-by-step approach is called desensitization. Systematic desensitization is an evidence-based therapy approach that combines relaxation techniques with gradual exposure to help you slowly overcome a phobia. During systematic desensitization, also called graduated exposure therapy, you work your way up through levels of fear, starting with the least fearful exposure. Desensitization allows patient to gradually challenge his/her fears, build confidence, and master skills to controlling panic. According the Association of America there are eight steps to overcoming Your fear of flying:
  • 1.Identify the triggers that set you off. Figure out what frightens you and examine how your anxiety is triggered. Your goal is to identify particular triggers, so you can manage your dear when anxiety levels are low.
  • 2. Step onto the airplace with knowledge. Anxiety thrives on ignorance, and feeds off "what if?" catastrophic thoughts. But once you become knowledgeable, your "what if?" thoughts are limited by the facts. Become familiar with the facts. They will not eliminate your anxiety, but they will help you manage it.
  • 3. Anticipate your anxiety. Anticipatory anxiety is what we experience in anticipation of a fear. It is ofen the most intense anxiety, you will experience during your fligh, but it is not an accurage predictor of how you will feel on the flight.
  • 4. Separate fear from danger. It is often difficult to separate anxiety from danger because your body reacts in exactly the same way to both. Be sure to label your fear as anxiety, and remind yourself that feeling anxious does not mean your in in danger.
  • 5. Recognize that common sense makes no sense. Anxiety will trick you into thinking you are in danger when you are perfectly safe. Your gut feelings in these instances will always tell you to avoid, but if you follow these feelings, you will always be reinforcing your anxiety.
  • 6. Smooth over things that go bump in the flight. To manage anxiety when turbulence hits, learn about airplanes and how they are designed to handle turbulence. Focus on managing your anxiety, rather than when the turbulence will end or how severe it might get.
  • 7. Educate fellow fliers how to help you. other fliers need to know what frightens you, along with what helps you most to cope with anxiety during a flight.
  • 8. Value each flight. Exposure is the active ingredient in overcoming your fear. Every flight provides you with the opportunity to make the next one easier. Your goal is to retrain your brain to become less sensitized to the triggers that set you off.
